# DroneRL — Experimental Log

This document records the experiments I ran to compare the three RL algorithms,
including the ones whose results were not what I expected. The goal is to
make the reasoning behind the README's quantitative claims auditable and to
record the tension between "the polished story" and "what the data actually
shows."

All raw chart artifacts referenced here live in `data/analysis/` and are
regenerated by:

```bash
uv run python -m analysis.multi_seed_robustness
uv run python -m analysis.alpha_decay_sweep
```

The two scripts share `analysis/_runner.py` for the training loop so the
configurations are guaranteed identical across experiments.

---

## Background

Three algorithms, all tabular, on the same 12×12 grid world with stochastic
wind drift:

- **Bellman** — single Q-table, *constant* learning rate α.
- **Q-Learning** — single Q-table, α decays geometrically each episode
  (`α ← max(α_min, α · decay)`).
- **Double Q-Learning** — two tables QA, QB; on each step, one is updated
  using the other's value at its own arg-max action. Decaying α.

The standard textbook story is: under noisy environments and the max-operator
overestimation bias, Double-Q ≥ Q-Learning ≥ Bellman. Watkins (1989) proves
Q-Learning convergence requires Σα_t = ∞ and Σα_t² < ∞ — which a constant α
violates — so Bellman should fail under noise. Hado van Hasselt (2010) shows
Double-Q removes the upward bias in the TD target by decorrelating the action
selection and value estimation.

I wanted to check whether that ordering actually shows up in this specific
environment, and how robust the conclusion is.

---

## Hypothesis 1 — Bellman fails under noise

> **H1.** With constant α=0.7 in a stochastically-windy 12×12 grid, Bellman's
> last-200-episode mean reward will be *clearly* worse than Q-Learning and
> Double-Q.

**Method.** `analysis/multi_seed_robustness.py`, 5 seeds (3, 7, 11, 17, 23),
1500 episodes per algorithm, fixed board parameters (noise=0.5, density=0.12,
difficulty=0.3). Same dynamic-board seed shapes the hazard layout per run.

**Result.**

| Algorithm | Per-seed last-200 means | Spread (max − min) |
|-----------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| Bellman | 74.6, 74.5, 75.8, 74.8, 75.8 | **1.4** |
| Q-Learning | 74.4, 76.3, 75.7, 75.3, 75.6 | **1.8** |
| Double-Q | -184.5, -195.3, 74.7, 76.2, 76.0 | **271.5** |

**H1 verdict — REJECTED at this difficulty / training budget.** Bellman
performs *as well as* Q-Learning when the board is at medium difficulty,
contrary to the textbook prediction that constant α should diverge under
noise. Inspecting the seed=3 and seed=7 runs for Double-Q shows the surprise
is not on Bellman's side at all — it's that **Double-Q is the one with the
huge variance**, not Bellman.

**What this means for the README.** The original "Bellman struggles" framing
in the README's Scenario 1 section is *only* true at higher noise/difficulty
(Scenario 2: noise=0.95, difficulty=0.55). At the medium level, Bellman is
basically as good as the more sophisticated methods. The honest summary is
that the "noise breaks Bellman" claim needs to be qualified by the noise
*level* — it's not a switch, it's a slope.

## Hypothesis 2 — Double-Q's advantage is robust to its decay rate

> **H2.** Double-Q's superiority over Q-Learning should hold across a range
> of alpha_decay values, not depend on a precisely tuned decay.

**Method.** `analysis/alpha_decay_sweep.py`. Sweep `alpha_decay` over
{0.999, 0.9993, 0.9995, 0.9997, 0.9999, 1.0} for both Q-Learning and
Double-Q. 3 seeds per cell (3, 11, 23), same medium-difficulty board,
1500 episodes. Bellman included as a horizontal reference (no decay).

**Result (last-200 mean reward, average over 3 seeds; SEM in parentheses):**

| `alpha_decay` | Q-Learning | Double-Q |
|---------------|------------|----------|
| 0.9990 | 78.0 (±0.4) | -105.2 (±91.6) |
| 0.9993 | 76.7 (±0.8) | -198.4 (±3.8)  |
| 0.9995 | 77.4 (±0.5) | -11.5  (±89.3) |
| 0.9997 | 76.5 (±1.5) | -198.7 (±4.3)  |
| 0.9999 | 77.9 (±0.4) | -11.6  (±89.3) |
| 1.0000 | 76.4 (±1.7) | -110.3 (±92.6) |

Bellman reference (no decay): **77.5**.

**H2 verdict — REJECTED at this training budget.** Q-Learning is *extremely*
flat across the entire decay grid (76.4 to 78.0, a ~2-point window — the
SEM bars overlap each other). Double-Q is the opposite: catastrophically
unstable, with means swinging from −198 to −11, and high SEM (~90) because
some seeds converge while others diverge. The advantage of Double-Q is
*not* present at this episode budget regardless of decay.

**What this means.** Q-Learning's α-decay is doing surprisingly little here —
the agent reaches the same plateau even with `decay=1.0` (i.e. constant α at
α_start). Either the noise level isn't high enough to need decay, or 1500
episodes are enough for the value-iteration to settle even without it.
This is a real finding I would have missed without the sweep.

## Hypothesis 3 — Double-Q needs more episodes to stabilise

> **H3.** Double-Q's poor multi-seed performance at 1500 episodes is a
> training-budget effect, not a fundamental property: with longer training
> the catastrophic seeds will recover.

**Method.** Indirect — the existing `scripts/generate_comparison_charts.py`
runs Scenario 2 at 6,000 episodes. Cross-referencing those results with the
multi-seed runs lets me check whether more episodes flip the picture.

**Result.** In the 6,000-episode Scenario 2 (noise=0.95, difficulty=0.55,
seed=7), Double-Q ends at the lowest σ and the highest mean — matching the
textbook ordering. In the 1500-episode multi-seed run on medium difficulty,
Double-Q on seed=7 ends at **−195**.

**H3 verdict — TENTATIVELY SUPPORTED.** Same algorithm, same seed, vastly
different result based on how long it gets to train and how hard the board
is. The pattern is consistent with "Double-Q's bias correction needs enough
samples to dominate the higher initial variance from the QA/QB split", which
is what the theory says. It is *not* proved by this experiment alone — that
would need a sweep over episode count, which I haven't run.

---

## What I'd do next

The experiments above are enough to make the README's qualitative claims
honest, but a more thorough study would:

1. **Sweep episode budget** — run each algorithm at 500, 1500, 5000, 15000
   episodes and plot final reward vs budget. Confirms or refutes H3.
2. **Sweep noise level** — fix everything else, vary `noise_level` from 0.0
   to 1.0, plot the gap between Bellman and Q-Learning. Would directly
   measure where in noise-space Bellman's constant-α actually starts to
   matter.
3. **Bootstrap confidence intervals** for the alpha-decay points instead
   of SEM, since the per-seed distributions are clearly bimodal (some
   seeds converge, others don't).

I'm calling these out as future work rather than pretending they're done.

---

## Limitations

- **3–5 seeds is the bare minimum** for what's claimed here. Published RL
  papers typically use 10–30. The qualitative picture above is unlikely to
  flip with more seeds, but the SEMs would tighten.
- **Single board difficulty per experiment.** The multi-seed run uses one
  fixed board configuration; conclusions there don't automatically transfer
  to other difficulty levels.
- **No formal hypothesis testing.** I'm reporting means and SEMs, not p-values
  — appropriate for an exploratory write-up but not for a competitive
  benchmark claim.
